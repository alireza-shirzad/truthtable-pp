\subsection{Fingerprinting}

A string fingerprint can be understood as the answer vector to a small
collection of yes/no questions asked of a string. In the most precise
form one could imagine, there would be one such question per
\emph{atomic feature} of interest---for instance, ``does this string
contain the letter \texttt{a}?'', ``does it contain the bigram
\texttt{th}?'', ``does it start with a digit?'', or any other Boolean
property one cares to evaluate. Evaluating every atomic feature gives a
lossless, but potentially very large, summary of the string.

The fingerprint trades this precision for size. Instead of storing one
bit per atomic feature, we partition the features into $n$ groups and
\emph{or}~them together: a single bit fires whenever \emph{any} of its
constituent features fires. Each bit of the fingerprint is therefore
not a single feature but the disjunction of many features. This
compression is what makes fingerprints small and fast to evaluate---and
also what makes them lossy. The design problem is then twofold: choose
the family of atomic features to draw from, and choose how to group
them into bits.

Let $\mathcal{F} = \{f_1, f_2, \dots, f_m\}$ be a finite family of
\emph{atomic features}, where each $f_i$ is a Boolean function on
strings:
\begin{equation*}
  f_i : \Sigma^{*} \to \{0,1\}.
\end{equation*}
We make no assumption about what each $f_i$ computes; it may test for
the presence of a character, the presence of an $n$-gram, a positional
condition, the output of a hash function, or any other property.

A fingerprint of bitwidth $n$ is induced by a partition
$\{B_1, \dots, B_n\}$ of $\mathcal{F}$. The $j$-th bit of the
fingerprint of a string $s$ is the disjunction of the features assigned
to bin $B_j$:
\begin{equation}
  \Vector{f}_s[j] \;=\; \bigvee_{f \in B_j} f(s).
  \label{eq:bit-as-or}
\end{equation}
The full fingerprint $\Vector{f}_s \in \{0,1\}^n$ is the vector of these
disjunctions.

\paragraph{Example.} Let $\Sigma = \{a, e, h, l, n, t, u\}$ and take
the family $\mathcal{F} = \{f_a, f_e, f_h, f_l, f_n, f_t, f_u,
g_{\text{th}}, g_{\text{ut}}\}$, where $f_c(s) = \mathbb{1}[c \in s]$
and $g_{xy}(s) = \mathbb{1}[xy \text{ occurs in } s]$. With bitwidth
$n = 3$ and partition $B_1 = \{f_a, f_l, f_u\}$, $B_2 = \{f_e, f_h,
f_n, f_t\}$, $B_3 = \{g_{\text{th}}, g_{\text{ut}}\}$, the
fingerprints of a few strings are:
\begin{center}
  \begin{tabular}{lccc}
    \toprule
    String & $\Vector{f}[1]$ & $\Vector{f}[2]$ & $\Vector{f}[3]$ \\
    \midrule
    \texttt{utn}   & 1 & 1 & 1 \\
    \texttt{lute}  & 1 & 1 & 1 \\
    \texttt{heat}  & 1 & 1 & 0 \\
    \texttt{ten}   & 0 & 1 & 0 \\
    \bottomrule
  \end{tabular}
\end{center}
For instance, \texttt{lute} sets $\Vector{f}[3] = 1$ via the bigram \texttt{ut};
the fingerprint records that \emph{some} feature in $B_3$ fired, but
not which.


\subsection{Approximate Filtering}

Fingerprints become useful when we want to quickly decide whether a
candidate string $s$ could possibly contain a pattern $\zeta$, without
performing the full containment test.

\paragraph{The pruning rule.} For a pattern $\zeta$ and a candidate string
$s$, the fingerprint check accepts $s$ iff
\begin{equation}
  \Vector{f}_\zeta \subseteq \Vector{f}_s
  \;\;\Longleftrightarrow\;\;
  \forall j \in [n]:\; \Vector{f}_\zeta[j] = 1 \Rightarrow \Vector{f}_s[j] = 1.
  \label{eq:subset-check}
\end{equation}
For this check to be a sound filter---never rejecting a true
match---the feature family $\mathcal{F}$ must satisfy a
\emph{monotonicity} condition: whenever $\zeta$ is contained in $s$ in the
sense the query intends, every feature satisfied by $\zeta$ must also be
satisfied by $s$,
\begin{equation}
  \zeta \sqsubseteq s \;\;\Longrightarrow\;\; \forall f \in \mathcal{F}:\;
  f(\zeta) \le f(s).
  \label{eq:monotonicity}
\end{equation}
Under~\eqref{eq:monotonicity}, the subset
check~\eqref{eq:subset-check} cannot produce false negatives.

\paragraph{Where false positives come from.} The converse of the
subset check fails because of the \emph{or}-compression
in~\eqref{eq:bit-as-or}. A string $s$ may light bit $j$ via a feature
$f \in B_j$ unrelated to those the pattern requires; the fingerprint
cannot tell which feature of $B_j$ caused the bit to fire. Two design
choices govern how often this happens:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item The \textbf{feature family} $\mathcal{F}$. Richer families
  (e.g., $n$-grams, positional features) carry more information per
  atomic feature and therefore tolerate coarser bin assignments, at
  the cost of a larger $|\mathcal{F}|$ and a more expensive
  partition-selection problem.
  \item The \textbf{partition} $\{B_1, \dots, B_n\}$. Grouping
  features the workload needs to \emph{distinguish} into the same bin
  destroys that information; grouping interchangeable features wastes
  little.
\end{enumerate}

\paragraph{Example.} Reusing the setup from above, take pattern $\zeta =
\texttt{utn}$ with $\Vector{f}_\zeta = (1, 1, 1)$. Then \texttt{lute} passes the
filter as a false positive (its $\Vector{f}[3]$ bit is lit by \texttt{ut} rather
than by \texttt{utn}), while \texttt{heat} is correctly rejected on
$\Vector{f}[3]$ and \texttt{ten} on $\Vector{f}[1]$. Replacing $B_3$ with another
character bin would weaken the filter on \texttt{heat}; merging
\texttt{u} and \texttt{t} into one bin would weaken it on
\texttt{ten}.


Now building on this tool, we design a PIOP where 

\begin{DefinePIOP}{Approximate Filtering Check}{approx-filer}
    \begin{enumerate}[nosep]
        \item $\PIOPProver$ and $\PIOPVerifier$ compute the fingerprint of the pattern $\zeta$ as $\Vector{f}_\zeta$ and the field element encoding of the fingerprint as $f_\zeta=\sum_{j=0}^{n-1} 2^j \Vector{f}_\zeta[j]$.
        \item $\PIOPProver$ divides the fingerprint col
    \end{enumerate}
\end{DefinePIOP}

\subsection{Exact Filtering}

Let $\col$ be a string column in the database. We encode $\col$ in as a tuple of $8$ columns, $( \mathsf{h},\mathsf{b},\mathsf{l},\mathsf{a_{hb}}, \mathsf{d}, \mathsf{src}, \mathsf{a_d}, \mathsf{s_b})$, with the following semantics:
\begin{itemize}[nosep]
    \item Hash column $\mathsf{h}$ is of the same length as $\col$ containing the hash of each string in $\col$.
    \item Length column $\mathsf{l}$ is of the same length as $\col$, with $\mathsf{l}[i]$ being the length $|\col[i]|$ of the $i$-th string in $\col$.
    \item Data column $\mathsf{d}$ is of the size $\sum_{s \in \col} |s|$, containing the concatenation of all strings in $\col$.
    \item Source column $\mathsf{src}$ is of the same length as $\mathsf{d}$, with $\mathsf{src}[j]$ being the index $i$ of the string in $\col$ that the $j$-th character of $\mathsf{d}$ belongs to.
    \item Boundary column $\mathsf{b}$ is a column of the same length as $\col$ with $\mathsf{b}[i]$ being the index of the first character of the $i$-th string in $\col$ in the data column $\mathsf{d}$.
    \item The data activator column $\mathsf{a_d}$ is of the same length as $\mathsf{d}$, with $\mathsf{a_d}[i]$ being $1$ if the $i$-th character in $\mathsf{d}$ is an active chatacter of the column $\col$.
    \item The hash/boundry activator column $\mathsf{a_{hb}}$ is of the same length as $\col$, with $\mathsf{a_{hb}}[i]$ being $1$ if the $i$-th string in $\col$ is active.
    \item The boundary selector column $\mathsf{s_b}$ is of the same length as $\mathsf{b}$, with $\mathsf{s_b}[i]$ being $1$ if the $i$-th boundary in $\mathsf{b}$ is active.
    \end{itemize}

\subsubsection{Activator Consistency Check}
The \emph{Activator Consistency Check} takes the source column $\mathsf{src}$, the data activator $\mathsf{a_d}$, the length column $\mathsf{l}$, and the string activator $\mathsf{a_h}$, and proves that $\mathsf{a_d}$ and $\mathsf{a_h}$ are consistent: for every string, the number of its active characters equals its length when the string is active, and is $0$ otherwise. Equivalently, for every string index $i$,
\begin{displaymath}
  \mathsf{a_h}[i]\cdot\mathsf{l}[i] \;=\; \sum_{j:\,\mathsf{src}[j]=i} \mathsf{a_d}[j].
\end{displaymath}
We package this as the relation
\begin{displaymath}
\NPRelation_{\mathsf{ActCons}} = \left\{\,
  \NPInstance = \bot,\;
  \NPInstancey = (\Oracle{\MLE{\mathsf{src}}}, \Oracle{\MLE{\mathsf{a_d}}}, \Oracle{\MLE{\mathsf{l}}}, \Oracle{\MLE{\mathsf{a_h}}}),\;
  \NPWitness = (\mathsf{src}, \mathsf{a_d}, \mathsf{l}, \mathsf{a_h})
  \;\middle|\;
  \forall i,\; \mathsf{a_h}[i]\cdot\mathsf{l}[i] = \!\!\!\sum_{j:\,\mathsf{src}[j]=i}\!\!\! \mathsf{a_d}[j]
\,\right\}.
\end{displaymath}
The check is exactly a group-by-sum: grouping the rows $(\mathsf{src}, \mathsf{a_d})$ by $\mathsf{src}$ and summing $\mathsf{a_d}$ within each group must reproduce $\mathsf{l}$ on the active strings (those marked by $\mathsf{a_h}$). Treating $(\mathsf{src}, \mathsf{a_d})$ as an input table grouped by its key and $(\mathsf{ind}, \mathsf{l})$ with activator $\mathsf{a_h}$ as the grouped output, this is discharged by a single \cref{piop:keyedsumcheck}.
\begin{DefinePIOP}{Activator Consistency Check}{act-cons}
  \emph{Inputs:} $\Oracle{\MLE{\mathsf{src}}}, \Oracle{\MLE{\mathsf{a_d}}}, \Oracle{\MLE{\mathsf{l}}}, \Oracle{\MLE{\mathsf{a_h}}}$.
  \begin{enumerate}[nosep]
    \item $\PIOPProver$ and $\PIOPVerifier$ locally form the input table $\Left$ with key column $\Left.\key = \mathsf{src}$, value column $\Left.\val = \mathbf{1}$, and activator $\Left.\act = \mathsf{a_d}$ (so the keyed sum counts the active characters of each string).
    \item $\PIOPProver$ and $\PIOPVerifier$ locally form the output table $\Right$ with key column $\Right.\key = \mathsf{ind}$ (the index column, $\mathsf{ind}[i] = i$), value column $\Right.\val = \mathsf{l}$, and activator $\Right.\act = \mathsf{a_h}$.
    \item $\PIOPProver$ and $\PIOPVerifier$ invoke \cref{piop:keyedsumcheck} on $\Left$ and $\Right$, which forces $\sum_{j:\,\mathsf{src}[j]=i}\mathsf{a_d}[j] = \mathsf{a_h}[i]\cdot\mathsf{l}[i]$ for every key $i$.
  \end{enumerate}
\end{DefinePIOP}

    \subsubsection{Prefix Check} In a prefix check protocol, the verifier is given a tuple of the form $( \mathsf{h},\mathsf{b},\mathsf{l},\mathsf{a_h^{old}}, \mathsf{d}, \mathsf{src}, \mathsf{a_d^{old}}, \mathsf{s_b})$  and new activators $\mathsf{a_h^{new}}$ and $\mathsf{a_d^{new}}$. The prover needs to convince the verfier that the tuple $( \mathsf{h},\mathsf{b},\mathsf{l},\mathsf{a_h^{new}}, \mathsf{d}, \mathsf{src}, \mathsf{a_d^{new}}, \mathsf{s_b})$ is the result of applying a prefix filter $\mathsf{str}\%$ on the original tuple where $\mathsf{str}$ is the prefix string of length $k$.

   \begin{DefinePIOP}{Prefix Check}{prefix-check}
    \begin{enumerate}[nosep]
        \item $\PIOPProver$ sends two masks: a per-string mask $\mathsf{m_h}$ and a per-character mask $\mathsf{m_d}$, where $\mathsf{m_h}[i]=1$ iff $\mathsf{l}[i] < k$, and $\mathsf{m_d}[j]=1$ iff the string $\mathsf{src}[j]$ containing the $j$-th character has length less than $k$. These mark exactly the strings too short to admit a prefix of length $k$.
        \item $\PIOPProver$ and $\PIOPVerifier$ invoke \cref{piop:act-cons} on $(\mathsf{src}, \mathsf{m_d}, \mathsf{l}, \mathsf{m_h})$, which forces $\mathsf{m_d}$ to be the per-character broadcast of $\mathsf{m_h}$ (so the two masks are consistent).
        \item $\PIOPProver$ and $\PIOPVerifier$ check that every active entry of $\mathsf{m_h}$ marks a short string, i.e.\ $\mathsf{l}[i] < k$ for every $i$ with $\mathsf{m_h}[i]=1$, by a \cref{piop:lookup-check} of the $\mathsf{m_h}$-active entries of $\mathsf{l}$ against the table $\{0,1,\dots,k-1\}$.
        \item $\PIOPProver$ and $\PIOPVerifier$ define the masked old activators
        \begin{align*}
          \mathsf{a_d^{mid}} := \mathsf{a_d^{old}}\cdot(1-\mathsf{m_d}), \qquad
          \mathsf{a_h^{mid}} := \mathsf{a_h^{old}}\cdot(1-\mathsf{m_h}),
        \end{align*}
        which deactivate every string of length less than $k$. \emph{In all subsequent steps, $\mathsf{a_d^{mid}}$ and $\mathsf{a_h^{mid}}$ are used in place of $\mathsf{a_d^{old}}$ and $\mathsf{a_h^{old}}$.}
        \item $\PIOPProver$ and $\PIOPVerifier$ invoke \cref{piop:act-cons} on $(\mathsf{src}, \mathsf{a_d^{new}}, \mathsf{l}, \mathsf{a_h^{new}})$ to check that the new activators are consistent.
        \item $\PIOPProver$ and $\PIOPVerifier$ compute perform $k$ instances of  \cref{piop:zero-check} on the following columns:
        \begin{align*}
          \left[(\mathsf{d}-\mathsf{str}[i],\mathsf{s_b^i}\cdot \mathsf{a_d^{new}}\mathsf{a_d^{old}})\right]_{i=0}^{k-1}
        \end{align*}
        \item $\PIOPProver$ computes the all active predicate columns $[\mathsf{p}_i]_{i=0}^{k-1}$ such that 
       \begin{align*}
        \mathsf{p}_i[j]= \begin{cases}
        1 & \text{if } \mathsf{d}[j]=\mathsf{str}[i] \text{ and } \mathsf{s_b^i}[j]=1 \text{ and } \mathsf{a_d^{new}}[j]=1 \text{ and } \mathsf{a_d^{old}}[j]=1\\
        0 & \text{otherwise} 
        \end{cases}
       \end{align*} 
       \item $\PIOPProver$ and $\PIOPVerifier$ perform $k$ instances of \cref{piop:zero-check} on the following columns:
       \begin{align*}
          \left[(\mathsf{d}-\mathsf{str[i]},\mathsf{p}_i)\right]_{i=0}^{k-1}
        \end{align*}
        \item $\PIOPProver$ and $\PIOPVerifier$ perform $k$ instances of \cref{piop:nozero-check} on the following columns:
       \begin{align*}
          \left[(\mathsf{d}-\mathsf{str[i]},(1-\mathsf{p}_i)\cdot \mathsf{s_b^i} \cdot \mathsf{a_d^{new}}\mathsf{a_d^{old}})\right]_{i=0}^{k-1}
        \end{align*}
        \item For $i=1,\ldots,k-1$, $\PIOPProver$ rotates $\mathsf{p}_i$ by $i$, denotes the rotated column as $\mathsf{p}_i'$, and sends $\mathsf{p}_i'$ to $\PIOPVerifier$. Index $0$ needs no rotation, so $\mathsf{p}_0'=\mathsf{p}_0$.
        \item $\PIOPProver$ and $\PIOPVerifier$ define the dropped-boundary selector
        \begin{align*}
          \mathsf{s_b'} \;:=\; (1-\mathsf{a_d^{new}})\cdot \mathsf{a_d^{old}}\cdot \mathsf{s_b}
        \end{align*}
        and perform a zerocheck on the following column:
        \begin{align*}
          \left(\prod_{i=0}^{k-1}\mathsf{p}_i',\;\mathsf{s_b'}\right)
        \end{align*}
    \end{enumerate}
\end{DefinePIOP}
